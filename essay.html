Essay 1

During my tertiary education experiences, I have come to the realisation that I take several practices, ideas, and theories for granted. Honestly, I do not think I have ever acknowledged a good user interface or commended a good experience when interacting with a website. However, I have always vented my frustrations when website interface elements were confusing.

A website’s user interface (UI) comprises of the interactable elements that allow the user to navigate through the website and/or communicate certain information to the user. This includes, but is not limited to, navigation icons (hamburger button, gear cogs, etc.), search bars, on-screen buttons, and so on. Each website’s UI is built according to a certain layout.

User Experience (UX) on the other hand, refers to the quality of the interaction between the UI and the user. A positive UX can be achieved through various concepts and ideas such as the use of familiar icons, responsive and efficient websites, and allowing the user to reach the information that they require as soon as possible.

Now, both of these definitions are fairly vague, however, by the end of this essay, I hope to explore the ideas further by analysing the SuperSport website (https://supersport.com/.). I have interacted with this website for several years, and as technology evolved, the website evolved with it. SuperSport is also one of the oldest sports broadcasting channels in South Africa and being an individual that is very passionate about sports, I felt it was rather fitting to discuss how this particular website has built its user interface to achieve a positive user experience.

The article, The Basic Principles of User Interface Design by UXPin, discuss several ideas surrounding a good UX. One of them is, “placing the user at the centre” (UXPin, 2020). A major part of the SuperSport website is publishing news articles. It is important to note that, unlike sports-specific websites like LiveScore (https://www.livescore.com/en/.), or RugbyPass (https://www.rugbypass.com/news/.), SuperSport publishes articles, press conferences, highlights, results and fixtures of a variety of different sports. They achieve this centring of the user by displaying articles consistently across the UI. Each article has a title, a category and a timestamp which allows the user to navigate to an article that is relevant to them. The articles are also displayed from the most recent to the older articles, which ensures that the reader is reading relevant news.

In the screenshot above, several UI design choices are made to display certain information to the user to create a positive user experience. In another article by UXPin, several Web Developers and Designers discuss aspects of good UI design (UXPin, 2021). Weber discusses “consistency and clarity” as two crucial elements of a good UI (UXPin, 2021). When a mouse/trackpad cursor, hovers over an article, a shadow surrounds the article and the image zooms in slightly. This features creates a positive user experience as it clearly shows which article is being hovered over, however, certain faces or text may be cut out slightly. This is an example of good intention but could create a negative experience as certain players could be indistinguishable. Again, this is dependent on execution, if the website is tested efficiently by the web developers, this should not be a problem. The triangle pointing to the right and is surrounded by a circle or a ‘play button,’ communicates that there is a video or audio aspect involved in the article.

In terms of consistency and clarity, the user can see where they are on the website. Under “Football” on the navigation tab in Annexure 1, the user can see a white line showing which tab they are on. This could be clearer if a different colour is used like the dark red in the SuperSport logo to keep within the colour scheme, but the user’s position is still being communicated. Also, the football tab is made up of different sections (Home, Fixtures, Results, and so on), and the website effectively communicates that the user is in the Home section. To summarise, in the image, the user is in the home section of the football tab. Each section, be it Rugby, Cricket, or Golf, is illustrated in this same way. This consistency allows the user who is interested in multiple sports to navigate through each sports section easily.

Upon searching for some of my frequently visited websites to base this essay on, I pushed a few to the side due to the excessive number of advertisements present on the websites. Advertisements play a huge role in negative user experience. They often draw attention away from the purpose of websites or delay the user from getting the necessary information. However, there are several ways to optimise a website to ensure that advertisements are not breaking the website. Google uses “My Ad Control” to allow users to control what ads they see and why through settings (Finn, 2022). Now, for many websites, this is not possible. Google is a major search engine that is visited frequently. SuperSport on the other hand is visited on a need-to-know basis. A user wants to know the result of a specific match, they search for SuperSport and navigate through the website. I do not think that web developers need to go to such a far extent to ensure their site is optimised for each user. We can work with what we know. SuperSport is a sports broadcasting and news website, if a user accesses this website, they have an interest in sports. This is already one factor to consider when choosing advertisements. Another process could be implementing surveys. Ask the user to take a survey to ensure that the advertisements are relevant to the user. In Annexure 1, we see that there is one advertisement per section. The user sees the navigation bar, and then an advertisement; the first set of news articles, and again an advertisement. This does draw the attention away from the website slightly, but it is not as frustrating as multiple pop-up advertisements that force the user to interact with them.

The quick access bar in the footer section of the UI also creates a positive UX. It allows the user to access all parts of the website without having to click and search through each tab. It also acts as a summary for SuperSport as it shows all the information available on the website.

Small changes and elements that emphasize interface objects play a major role in the user experience. Highlighting selected text and changing the cursor from an arrow cursor to an I Beam when input is available or to a hand cursor when there is a link creates clear communication between the user and the UI. When a website is intuitive, it allows the user to gather the information they need easily.

Another positive aspect of the UX is that the website does not force you to external websites for news, highlights, etc. This allows the user to stay in an environment where they are comfortable and that they trust. Videos are played from different websites like YouTube; however, the videos can be enjoyed within the website. This also encourages users to return to the website as everything that they need is in one place and also ties in with the ethics of interactive design.

For the next part of my essay, I will be drawing on the ethical concerns that Peta Goldberg discusses in the Ethics Of UX Practice You, Your Audience And The Weight Of Experience lecture slides (Goldberg, 2021).

SuperSport gathers scores and results across several sports and displays them accordingly, however, the articles and interviews are still completed by humans. Therefore, the site uses modern technology to improve the efficiency in result gathering but does not eliminate the jobs of reporters. Was this improvement necessary? Yes. At the time of writing, South Africa faces several power outages (load shedding) daily. People who cannot afford backup power supplies such as inverters, generators, and so on still should be able to keep up to date with the sports that they are passionate about. This improvement in score gathering allows users to access results and commentary much quicker. I do not think that removing the human aspect (“de-skilling”) from sports journalism will result in a positive user experience (Goldberg, 2021). Human ideas and thoughts add the edge that makes articles interesting for users. Similar to commentary, major sports commentators create a better experience for viewers.

DS-tv broadcasts the matches that SuperSport has access to. When navigating through the SuperSport website, there are mentions and links to signing up to DS-tv and their partner, Showmax. To some extent, this can be viewed as “nudging,” however, it does not force the user to the broadcasting sites (Goldberg, 2021). It is important to note that the goal of the many broadcasters and/or websites, is to make a profit. If there are any ways that they can give the user the option to access their channels to make a profit without forcing the users to pay or provide any information, it takes the form of indirect advertising rather than unethical design.

For a news website like SuperSport, the core of ethics surrounding it, is to upload objective articles that are well-researched and do not spread any false information. To ensure that this is standard practice, SuperSport needs to monitor the information published and communicate to the users if any information from previously published articles were incorrect. Taking accountability for mistakes or false information allows the website to build a good relationship with users and in turn create a better user experience.

I underestimated the amount of work and thought that went into designing a user interface. As an aspiring web developer, I am hoping that with time and experience, my website development skills will improve tremendously. Currently, I feel that I overdo certain aspects when a minimalistic display would be more effective. After going through several websites, I have realised that clear communication between the user and UI is more important than a website that is aesthetic with poor communication.

Exam Essay

Artificial Intelligence (AI) Models have taken the digital world by storm. It allows us to access information quickly and efficiently, without scouring through several websites. However, when technology advances rapidly, we are often blinded by its ease of use and do not thoroughly evaluate its risks and faults. We do not only overlook the validity of the information provided but also the intentions of the powerhouse tech companies/countries that release them. The Centre for AI Safety (CAIS) made a statement regarding the urgency of outlining, discussing, and analysing the potential risks that come with artificial intelligence (CAIS, 2023).

Looking at it from a broad perspective, several countries still do not have reliable internet access or are not completely technologically efficient yet. On the other hand, there are countries where the internet is accessible, however, their “percentage of internet penetration” is far lower than other countries (Graham & Sabbata & Zook 2015: 92). Regardless of their infrastructure or internet literacy, or lack thereof, the internet is advancing faster than the spread of accessible internet access. Developing and less developed countries are again being “left behind” and disregarded in the introduction and evolution of AI (Chatterjee & Dethlefs, 2022).

I have given a brief overview of the issues with the introduction of AI, now I would like to dive deeper into the statement the CAIS made in relation to The World in www and Digital Inequalities.

As a starting point in discussing how AI is an existential threat to the world, I would like to discuss how AI affects various employment procedures. This refers to the AI model’s ability to do a certain job, and to what extent it completes said job. For example, a language model like ChatGPT can generate, edit, and/or explain a code, that will carry out a desired outcome. The generation of this code already replaces a developer as it creates a starting point for individuals, companies, businesses, and so on. What stops individuals, companies, etc. from using this language model to create code and have fewer employees who only modify and implement the generated code into their particular environment? If this hypothetical scenario becomes a reality, this will lead to a drop in the employment rate for developers. We do not understand the capabilities of the self-learning models and cannot predict how quickly they may or may not evolve. Now this is one particular job that can be affected by AI language models. What about jobs that have already been affected by automated systems and self-learning AI language models? In an article published by Sensorium, they discuss the jobs that AI may replace such, as “customer support, receptionists, accountants,” and so on (Sensorium, 2023). The takeover has already begun. For example, if you have an issue with an item you bought online, you first go through several processes with a chatbot to solve this issue before you are finally referred to a customer support representative (a human being). In many instances, appointments and job applications are all operated by automated systems. And what stops individuals from providing an AI model with their invoices to generate the books of their business? In the same article, jobs like “CEOs, PR management,” and other managerial roles are regarded as irreplaceable (Sensorium, 2023). The average salary for CEOs in 2023 is over R1 million whereas the average salary for a receptionist is just over R90 000 (payscale, n.d.). This means that, in some cases, higher-income earners are less likely to be affected by AI in comparison to lower-income earners. Sadly, this is the reality of the introduction of artificial intelligence. This leads us to the question: if an individual is forced out of a job due to the evolution of AI, how are they expected to get promoted and work their way up their respective ladder to reach the higher-income jobs where they are irreplaceable?

Digital “bias” and discrimination are major issues with artificial intelligence self-learning models (Ferrer, van Nuenen, Such, Cot´e & Criado, 2020). These models are designed to learn from a wide pool of inputs, applications, questions, phrases, and so on, to develop an appropriate answer or analyse a “data set” and generate an output (Lee, Resnick & Barton, 2019). Due to several factors, including the above-mentioned lack of internet accessibility, these self-learning models may not be able to answer certain questions or prompts accordingly as they do not often interact with these groups of people enough to provide the necessary information. This gives individuals an unfair advantage over less privileged individuals as firstly, the tools are easily accessible to them and the information gathered is relative to questions which are commonly asked within their regions. Whereas, developing countries, which have a lower number of users of these language models, will interact with the AI models less.

To emphasize, AI self-learning models rely on trends and patterns to gain information and effectively answer prompts. However, this is also how stereotypes and societal norms are born and spread to discriminate against certain groups of people. In an article published by Brookings, there was a discrepancy with “facial recognition technology” as it did not effectively recognise “darker-skinned” individuals, and “women” (Lee et al., 2019). This was due to the fact that the “training data set” comprised a majority of white males. This is a major security risk for individuals of darker skin tones and “women” as facial recognition is an important security feature available on certain mobile phones (Lee et al., 2019). AI should not only be functional for a select group of individuals.

Evidently, there are a lot of unsolved errors, discrepancies and limitations with these AI learning models; however, it is still available to individuals who have access to them. Let us look at a rudimentary example of this situation: if a car is not ready or tested effectively to be sold to the mass market, it cannot be sold. Why? Because there might be potential dangers, unknown risks, structural issues, and so on. Like said cars, there are a lot of unknown risks and factors that have not been found regarding AI. Why is it allowed to be active on such a broad spectrum? We do not know its full potential nor do we understand the extent and rate of its evolution. This makes AI dangerous. On one hand, it may offend an individual or provide them with incorrect information; on the other hand, users are not able to see where all the information was gathered from. If information is gathered from a specific resource, phrased differently, and outputted as a response to the user, we are discrediting the work of individuals who spent hours, days or even years to research and create the piece of work.

In addition to the above-mentioned factors, artificial intelligence can also be invasive. If we look at advertisements displayed on devices, they rely on learning models. This means that your navigation path on search engines is monitored and stored to generate a suitable set of advertisements for each individual. This is described as efficient as it displays advertisements relevant to each person, however, it is storing information. Since these algorithms are generated to gain certain information, how can users be sure that these language models are not storing information that users do not want to be stored? This is another "what if?" question.

A major concern with regard to AI is that there are too many uncertainties. It may generate incorrect information, or it may make an entire job redundant. We do not fully understand the capabilities of the artificial intelligence learning models and individuals cannot be certain that using these models will have positive outcomes all the time. If there could be a risk of extinction or higher unemployment rates, is it actually worth developing something that could be so destructive to the human race? Both yes, and no. These tools make tasks easier and quicker, however, there are risks to using these tools. To some extent, it is too late. These tools are published already and are a part of the future. They are used in schools, universities, jobs, and several other environments. It would be more helpful to try and understand or grasp the extent of the self-learning tool’s intelligence and capabilities. Knowledge and understanding are one of the many ways to mitigate risks and create a digital environment free from digital inequality. An individual should be able to use these tools regardless of their race, sex, gender, and so on.

Now, the burning question: Do I believe that “mitigating the risk of extinction from AI should be a global priority alongside other societal-scale risks such as pandemics and nuclear war?” (CAIS, 2023). I do not think that there is a simple yes or no answer to this. I have presented various facts and scenarios that support this argument; however, I do think that to some extent that pandemics and nuclear war are a more recent concern than the dangers of artificial intelligence. I am aware that it is better to be prepared for something than to deal with unexpected digital catastrophes, however, the world is still recovering from COVID-19. Diseases and nuclear war often affect everyone regardless of their wealth or intelligence. If there are any sort of catastrophic risks that can be predicted with AI, the models should be removed, reworked, and republished when humans can be in full control. An employee of Google was placed on “paid leave” after he believed that a “chatbot” showed signs of emotions (de Urquia, 2022). Even though it was dismissed by Google, if AI develops emotions, it is important to compare it to a newborn living being that does not fully understand how to process and express itself effectively. And something so powerful whose intelligence may grow faster than their creators’ is an important factor when discussing the “risk of extinction from AI” (CAIS, 2023). If we do not acknowledge the evident risks, there will be consequences for this. It is a matter of dealing with issues effectively and working with different groups to ensure that digital inequalities are mitigated, and also we must question whether exceptionally effective automated systems are worth increasing the unemployment rate and poverty rate.
